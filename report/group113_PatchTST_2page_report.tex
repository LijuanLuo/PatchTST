\section{Introduction}

We re-implemented the supervised version of \textbf{PatchTST} from Nie et al., \emph{A Time Series is Worth 64 Words: Long-term Forecasting with Transformers}~\cite{nie2023patchtst}. The paper argues that earlier Transformer forecasters often underperformed simple linear baselines and that two changes --- \textbf{patching} and \textbf{channel-independence} --- make Transformers competitive again for long-horizon time-series forecasting. Our project had two goals: (1) reproduce the core supervised results of PatchTST, and (2) test whether the same architecture transfers to a practical business task: \textbf{retail demand forecasting} on Favorita grocery sales.

\section{Chosen Result}

We focused on two results from the paper:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Table 3}: supervised PatchTST/42 vs.\ DLinear vs.\ vanilla Transformer on ETTh1, ETTm1, and Weather.
  \item \textbf{Table 7}: ablation of patching and channel-independence.
\end{itemize}
These were chosen because they directly capture the paper's main claims: that PatchTST improves forecasting performance and that both architectural choices matter.

\section{Methodology}

\textbf{Implementation.} We implemented PatchTST in PyTorch from the paper, with RevIN, patch length $P{=}16$, stride $S{=}8$, look-back window $L{=}336$, and a shared Transformer encoder. Channel-independence is implemented by reshaping the input from $(B,L,M)$ to $(B\cdot M,L)$ so each channel is processed independently but with shared weights. We also implemented DLinear~\cite{zeng2023dlinear}, a vanilla Transformer baseline, and the ablation variants \texttt{P+CI}, \texttt{CI Only}, \texttt{P Only}, and \texttt{Original}.

\textbf{Datasets.} We used ETTh1, ETTm1, and Weather for replication, and the Favorita Grocery Sales dataset for the extension. For Favorita, we selected the largest Quito store and treated the 33 product families as channels.

\textbf{Training and metrics.} We used MSE loss, Adam, OneCycleLR, and early stopping. Results are reported with test MSE and MAE in standardized space, following the paper.

\textbf{Important implementation findings.} Cross-checking against the official code revealed three details that materially affected accuracy: (1) PatchTST/42 requires replication padding that produces 42 patches rather than 41; (2) the encoder uses BatchNorm rather than LayerNorm; and (3) DLinear uses shared weights across channels by default. Fixing these closed an early 5--15\% performance gap.

\section{Results \& Analysis}

\textbf{Benchmark replication.} Our results are best described as a \textbf{partial replication}. On \textbf{Weather}, PatchTST closely matched the paper and was best at all four horizons. On \textbf{ETTm1}, PatchTST also beat DLinear and the vanilla Transformer at all four horizons, but its absolute MSE remained above the paper. On \textbf{ETTh1}, PatchTST only partially matched the paper: it beat DLinear at horizon 96, but DLinear was slightly better at 192, 336, and 720.

\textbf{Seed sensitivity.} To test whether the ETT mismatch was just random initialization noise, we ran a 3-seed sweep on ETTh1 and ETTm1. The standard deviation across seeds was only 0.001--0.004, far smaller than the gap to the paper, which suggests the mismatch is systematic rather than random. We believe the remaining gap is due to implementation-sensitive training details and script-level hyperparameter overrides in the official repository.

\textbf{Ablation.} The full model (\texttt{P+CI}) performed best in all ablation settings. This supports the paper's central claim that \textbf{both} patching and channel-independence contribute to PatchTST's forecasting gains.

\textbf{Retail extension.} On Favorita, PatchTST achieved the best MSE at 7-day, 14-day, and 30-day horizons:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Horizon & PatchTST & DLinear & Transformer \\
\midrule
7d  & \textbf{0.940} & 1.302 & 2.736 \\
14d & \textbf{0.995} & 1.366 & 2.846 \\
30d & \textbf{0.930} & 1.424 & 2.527 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

This corresponds to a 27--35\% MSE reduction relative to DLinear and a much larger margin over the vanilla Transformer. Interestingly, the retail results were not strictly monotonic in horizon length: 30-day MSE was slightly lower than 14-day MSE. We interpret this as a dataset-specific effect driven by seasonality and window-level volatility rather than as a replication error.

\section{Reflections}

This project showed that \textbf{reproduction quality depends heavily on hidden details}. Even when the model design is correct, small implementation choices such as padding, normalization, and baseline defaults can materially change the results. A second lesson is that \textbf{matching the exact numbers in a paper is different from reproducing its main conclusions}. In our case, Weather matched closely, ETTm1 reproduced the correct ranking, and ETTh1 only partially matched --- but across these cases, the core value of PatchTST's architecture remained visible.

The extension to Favorita also suggests that PatchTST is practically useful beyond benchmark datasets. A natural next step would be to add exogenous retail variables such as promotions, holidays, and oil prices, since the current model is purely autoregressive.
